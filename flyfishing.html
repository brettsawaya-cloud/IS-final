Fly Fishing

On the water — where everything else disappears

What Is Fly Fishing?

Fly fishing is an angling method that uses a lightweight lure — called an artificial fly — to catch fish. Unlike conventional fishing where the weight of the lure carries the line, in fly fishing the weight of the line itself carries the fly to the target. It's a technique that demands patience, precision, and a deep connection with the natural environment.

Practiced for centuries across the rivers and streams of the world, fly fishing is as much about the experience as it is about the catch. Reading the water, matching the hatch, and perfecting the cast are skills developed over a lifetime.

A rainbow trout — the most iconic fly fishing target in the American West.

Essential Gear

Getting set up for fly fishing doesn't have to be overwhelming. Here's a breakdown of the core equipment every angler needs, along with the options you'll find within each category.

Your Starter Kit — In Order of Importance:

  1. Fly Rod — The foundation of your setup. Rods are rated by weight (1–14). For trout fishing, a 5-weight is the gold standard.
    • Beginner: Orvis Encounter, Redington Minnow
    • Intermediate: Sage Foundation, Echo Base
    • High-end: Scott Radian, Sage X, G. Loomis NRX+
  2. Fly Reel — Holds the fly line and backing. For most trout fishing, the reel is mainly a line holder, though a quality drag system matters for bigger fish.
    • Budget: Redington Behemoth, Orvis Clearwater
    • Mid-range: Ross Animas, Abel SDS
    • Premium: Tibor, Hardy Ultralite MTX
  3. Fly Line — The heavy, coated line that carries your fly through the air. Weight-forward floating lines are the most versatile choice.
  4. Leader & Tippet — The clear monofilament that connects your fly line to the fly. Lighter tippet (5X, 6X) is used for smaller, more delicate flies.
  5. Flies — The artificial lure itself. There are thousands of patterns.
    • Dry flies — float on the surface, imitating adult insects
    • Nymphs — sink below the surface, imitating larval insects
    • Streamers — larger flies that imitate baitfish or leeches
    • Wet flies — fished just below the surface
  6. Waders & Wading Boots — Waterproof pants that let you wade into rivers for better positioning and access to fish.

Fly fishing the Rogue River, Oregon — classic Western trout water.

Core Techniques

There are a few main approaches to fly fishing. Dry fly fishing is the most exciting — you present a floating fly on the surface and watch the trout rise to take it. Nymphing is the most productive, fishing weighted flies below the surface where trout spend most of their time feeding. Streamer fishing uses larger flies that imitate baitfish, often producing the biggest fish. And matching the hatch — selecting a fly that imitates whatever insects are currently emerging — is the most satisfying puzzle the sport has to offer.
